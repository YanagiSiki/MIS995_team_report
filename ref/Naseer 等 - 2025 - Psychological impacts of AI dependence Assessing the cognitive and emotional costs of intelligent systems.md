ABSTRACT
The study explores the cognitive and emotional consequences of
dependence on Artificial Intelligence among health professionals and
educators in Punjab, Pakistan. As AI is increasingly being infused into
professional practice, it becomes important to study the psychological
impacts of such dependence to optimize the use of the technology. This
study used a quantitative research approach with a sample of 500 health
professionals and educators selected by simple random sampling. Data
was collected from self-reporting questionnaires specifically designed to
measure the cognitive and emotional consequences of AI usage. The
statistical analysis was carried out through correlation and regression
techniques and post-hoc analysis. Results: Various psychological
outcomes were found to be very significantly associated with AI usage.
The result showed that a long usage of an AI tool led to cognitive
overload, meaning mental exhaustion, and subsequently decreased
decision-making ability. Furthermore, the extent of AI usage was
correlated with enhanced emotional stress. Professionals stated
increased anxiety and pressure in critical decision-making as a result of
the dependency on AI. Excessive AI usage was also associated with short
attention span, therefore indicating a negative impact on the cognitive
involvement of professionals. Additionally, moderate emotional
dependence on AI systems was found, especially in health professionals
and educators working under stressful conditions. These findings reflect
the two-sided nature of the impact of AI: although AI systems contribute
to efficiency and proper decision-making, they also generate severe
cognitive and emotional challenges. The study points to the necessity of
integrating AI with considerations for mental well-being and provides
practical recommendations to balance out the psychological effects of
dependence on AI. Some of the outcomes include the designing of AI tools
that decrease the cognitive load, training on managing emotional
dependency, and guidelines on using AI in high-pressure environments.

1. Introduction

Since AI has developed to be such a strong and all-influential technology for numerous
professional activities, healthcare and education are undoubtedly two sectors for which AI visibly causes
to be more disturbed. As much as tools and systems working on AI are used within these domains more,
it is the benchmark of how professionals get things done, decide about how they go about doing so, or
with what they share an environment. AI technologies fundamentally change clinical practice, patient
care, and most administrative processes of healthcare. In the last decade, AI has transformed
diagnostics and has substantially improved the accuracy of diagnosis and treatment in the health care
system. One application which machine learning algorithms are being completely exercised is that of
computation, in estimating medical images analysis, showing an example image similar to an X-ray or
MRI/CT scan that diagnoses early onset symptoms of the cancer and estimates the rate of degradation
into the future, as caused to the patient (Esteva et al. 2017). It sometimes goes above and beyond a
human doctor in diagnosing the disease; evidence shows that such diagnostic accuracy goes higher,
specifically in image-based diagnoses (Ravì, et al., 2016). In addition, it is personalizing the treatment
because it allows massive data analytics in the greatest therapies possible that be applicable for the
patient such that treatment can be more streamlined and targeted for the disease process (Topol, 2019).

Administrators of hospitals and clinics are now using the AI in automating more routine
administrative functions such as patient scheduling, billing, and medical records. By automating such
clerical work, health professionals have more time to attend to the patient. More than this, AI
applications including chatbots, and virtual assistants are applied towards helping patients' health
management and scheduling as well as follow-ups, hence heightening patient involvement and
satisfaction (Jiang et al., 2017).

Thanks to the impacts of AI related to healthcare, through frequent usage by technology, there
have come many threats as many healthcare providers continue to rely upon AI for decisive decisions.
This lead to cognitive overload and stress if AI is used with a failed system or wrong suggestions.
Additionally, technological dehumanization among patients is also increasing, given that the traditional
physician-patient interaction methods have been lost in recent times (Huo et al., 2025). The increasing
reliance on AI systems for diagnosis and decision-making provokes questions on the erosion of human
judgment elements and the emotional elements of care that lie at the heart of the physician-patient
bond (Topol, 2019).

With artificial intelligence in education, there is an adoption process that changes how educators
teach while the learners learn also. For example, an AI adaptive learning system able to learn data
concerning every child. This system, through its processing and analysis ability, can also adjust what is
to be taught to fit each type of information a child at his or her capability may absorb by the process of
learning applied appropriately. This, therefore, not a single child is left behind because more student-
centered learning methods become effective. In AI, for students, this can be in real-time use as feedback
upon which learning results and engagement become improved (Gokhe et al., 2024). AI also frees time

### 1.1 Problem Statement

In terms of cognitive impact, AI systems process large amounts of data, which can overwhelm
professionals, leading to cognitive overload. For example, practitioners have strain in applying AI-based
recommendations within the clinical science that indeed cognitively taxing. An instructor creating AI
datasets with student feedback data may feel compelled by this knowledge to shift out of their thoughts
to make value choices. However, in all these situations, the thought behind dependence on AI is that it
diminishes critical thinking and problem-solving because the professionals depend more on what the
machine exposes than judgment (Weisberger, 2024). In reality, it faced by humans at their emotional
level on the stress factor and anxiety about dependence on artificial intelligence. Physicians are anxious
by AI-based diagnoses that do not work out just as desired due to errors happening. An instructor is
frustrated regarding automated grading with assessments because automated grading depersonalizes
or leads to an educator's burnout. It means the more reliance of AI decisions brings a sense of emotional
detachment towards their work, and then cannot develop similar feelings for patients or students
(Luxton, 2014). There is also a question of autonomy and professional identity tangled with dependence
on AI systems. In the areas of health and education, the more a professional relies on AI, the less they
might be in control of their work, which create emotional dissonance and reduce job satisfaction. The
huge scope for efficiency in AI means cognitive and emotional barriers that are pertinent to its
utilization have to be comprehended so as to tackle issues of professional well-being associated with it.

### 1.2 Significance of the Study

To optimize the AI systems as well as professionalism's welfare through its advancement,
psychological implications concerning AI addiction have to be realized by the profession. Although AI is
implemented for the sake of speed and the best decision-making capabilities, its impact can bring about
mental stress and emotional pain if not done smartly. These impacts, in turn, contribute to more
friendly AI systems that are useful for professionals who are able to ease their burden mentally and
clearly think about critical services like health and education services. Job satisfaction and mental
health also need to overcome emotional issues of stress, anxiety, and burnout. Taking into
consideration the emotional element related to dependence on AI, this research enable the designing of
systems that limit stress and ensure professional engagement with performance improvement and
reduced turnover. Therefore, the fruits of such research directed into best practices concerning AI
integration. The general goal to assist organizations in building fairer, support systems that aid
professionals without overburdening them. Better AI serve those who are being helped by the use of the
technology and also bring about better productivity in healthcare and education due to a healthier and
more productive workforce.

2. Literature Review

The last ten years have witnessed the integration of artificial intelligence into life at an
accelerated pace. Artificial intelligence has transformed the very composition of the way people's lives
and work patterns are designed by becoming central. The sectors in which AI has made the most
significant impact include health and education services sectors, which offer massive improvements in
efficiency, productivity, and decision-making. However, this is balanced by the much deeper concern for
cognitive and emotional implications of growing reliance on AI in these particular sectors. The present
literature review considers the contribution of AI to health and education, reports critical analyses of
key research studies about the effects of cognition-including overloads and reduced attention spans-on
the psychological consequences of emotional distress and dependence and follows the part AI takes in
mental health in the workplace.

AI changes fundamentally how people carry out their daily activities, particularly in health and
education fields. In healthcare, for instance, machine learning algorithms and even robotic systems
become integral parts in diagnosis processing, treatment plans, and in general, taking care of the
patient. In some ways, significant steps for AI have been shown in diagnostic imaging, where patterns
in medical data, such as CT scans, MRIs, and X-rays, may be identified by algorithms that machine
learning may miss out on (Esteva et al., 2017). Another excellent application of AI is the clinical decision
support system, which analyzes patient data and informs healthcare professionals on the available
treatment options, thus potentially improving the quality of care and minimizing errors in cases where
such systems exist (Jiang et al., 2017). The case is also no different in education. AI is transforming how
teaching and learning happen. This allow algorithms to tailor learning contents according to every
student's requirements: engagement becomes higher, and effects are also better (VanLehn, 2011). AI
tools allow the educators to follow up on real-time students' progress and help them to offer
appropriate instruction based on such an understanding. The automated grading and plagiarism
detection tool also reduce some administrative burden upon the teachers for them to use more time and
effort in the teaching process itself (Humble & Mozelius, 2022). However, the increasing dependability
on AI in professional setup has raised doubts about its consequences on the profession in terms of
cognitive and psychological impacts.

A common cognitive impairment associated with the use of AI is cognitive overloading. Cognitive
overload describes the situation that arises whenever there is an over-presented amount of information
resulting in confusion, fatigue, and poor decision-making abilities (Siregar, 2024). In healthcare, AI-
based systems produce copious amounts of data from patient records, medical imaging, and lab results.
Where AI might be of help analyzing such information, the sheer number of recommendations or alerts
produced by AI systems overwhelms a healthcare professional. This, according to research by Wysocki
et al. (2023), leads to decision fatigue in that clinicians are not able to make decisions as correctly or as
quickly after having seen large amounts of data. Such environments as emergency rooms or intensive
care units might have significant patient safety implications when cognitive overload happens.

Similarly, in education, the feeling of cognitive overload by the teachers may result from the use
of AI-based student performance tracking platforms. AI tools monitor student progress and flag at-risk
students to suggest interventions. The amount of data that the educators have to interpret is
tremendous. Halkiopoulos & Gkintoni (2024) suggest that if the teachers are exposed to such data, they
might get flooded with it, which makes it difficult to decide which students to pay attention to
immediately, thus leading to mental overload and poor teaching. Additionally, with the high usage of AI
in healthcare and education, the span of attention decreases. Attention fragmentation occurs through
switching between different tasks, for example, trying to interpret an AI output or answer system alerts
(George, 2024). With decreased focus, it becomes more difficult to develop deeper thinking critical,
which may help in healthcare to make important decisions in very complex situations.

Studies also show that over-reliance on AI systems can influence decision-making. In healthcare,
if professionals are overly dependent on AI-generated recommendations, they begin to lose faith in their
clinical judgment. Luxton (2014) concluded a study stating that healthcare professionals often rely on AI
recommendations; however, if the AI systems make mistakes, it can break their confidence in their
abilities and cause anxiety or second-guessing. Similarly, in school education, as teachers depend
increasingly on AI-powered grading or designing lessons, questions may arise that undermine their
professionalism in pedagogical practice which may further deplete innovation in the classroom settings
(Humble & Mozelius, 2022).

In addition to these, cognitive overloads are relevant, but equally important is looking at the
emotion-based effects AI has on professional workers. Emotional dependence on artificial systems is
constantly growing as artificial intelligence systems begin performing more decision-making tasks. For
example, in the medical field, a doctor may increasingly rely on the AI available to them to make
diagnoses and to evolve prescription plans, thereby moving a step away from himself from the process
of decision-making. This detachment probably influences the emotional bond a doctor might have with
a patient as it makes him less responsible for the decisions made by the AI (Topol, 2019). This
emotional detachment may also cause stress, especially in stressful settings where AI tools may break
down or give incorrect recommendations. If AI systems err in vital medical decisions, health workers
may be worried as they could face legal implications from mistakes that were not made by them
(Wysocki et al., 2023).

Increased role of AI also mounts pressure on education. Pressure might come upon the teachers
and educators using AI-based grading tools or student assessment tools for the accuracy and justice of
the automated grading systems. Humble and Mozelius (2022) have researched that when AI commits
errors in grading or fails to address individual variances of students, then teachers feel disempowered
and frustrated. Increased pressure to adopt AI technologies and keep up the pace of new innovations
may cause educators to burn out since, with all the challenges of balancing the teaching task, pressure
for the adaptation of educational technology remains (Halkiopoulos & Gkintoni, 2024).

More misery is supported by emotional reliance on AI. The higher AI becomes in making
decisions the more it makes professionals feel their expertise is worthless. Taking the healthcare
scenario for instance, if a health worker has to decide on a higher basis of machine-generated data than
his judgment, he or she become anxious (Luxton, 2014). Similarly, if AI-based solutions take over the
role of being the primary source of educational guidance for students in the opinion of teachers, then
emotional burnout may occur in educators (Gokhe et al., 2024). This pervasive use of AI-based
solutions probably undermines professionals' self-confidence. Such negative psychological effects can
influence job satisfaction, performance, and overall welfare.

The mental health of professionals interacting with AI also depends on their workplace

3. Research Design and Methodology

This part presented the research design and methodology that had been employed in carrying
out the study on the psychological effects of reliance on Artificial Intelligence (AI) among health
workers and teachers in Punjab, Pakistan. This was a quantitative research as it entailed a plan to
analyze the numerical data which could reveal mental and emotional influences related to the
increasing reliance on AI-based tools in the work environment. Regarding the systematization of the
approach on data collection as well as on analyses, there was thus robustness and reliability in the
study so that to ensure the integrity of the conclusions.

### 3.1 Research Type

The applied research design used the quantitative character of determination of the statistical
significance about the numerical data associated with the cognitive and emotive influences brought
about by AI to healthcare professionals and education professions. This only could happen in a
quantitative manner, measuring specifics in the variables for and through their statistical analysis,
establishing patterns and correlations and a connection between its application and psychical results.
Quantitative tools in the form of self-report questionnaires for collecting structured data to draw
further conclusions based on the statistical evidence obtained to portray an accurate image regarding
the cognitive and emotional implications arising from dependence on AI.

The study was able to deliver quantitative insight, thus having proved to be instrumental as far
as generalizing the results to a larger population of health care professionals and educators of Punjab
was concerned. The statistical analysis had also been able to provide an enhanced understanding about
the strength and direction of relations between AI usage patterns and psychological effects that
otherwise could not have been as easily attained through the qualitative methods.

### 3.3 Target Group

The target population of the study were precisely the health professionals and educators of
Punjab. There is a choice of these two professional groups in this study. The reason is that these
represent sectors in which AI integration has increased more and the psychological effects of
dependency on AI are still unexplored. Health care practitioners within the city or rural communities
encounter AI tools in support of making diagnostic and treatment decisions, office work, or monitoring
patient-related activities. For educators, there is now room for applying AI tools to supplement personal
learning needs, prepare the curriculum, or monitor performance from students.

Health practitioners and teachers were put in settings that allowed an in-depth look at the
influence of AI on cognition and emotions, which tends to differ within different professional situations.
The insights gained were indeed more profound, considering the role of AI for mental wellbeing within
the high stress, decision-sensitive context of the health sector, and within the more cognitively
challenging situation of education.

### 3.4 Sampling Technique

Getting the participants in this population consider a simple random sampling technique. Simple
random sampling is the kind of probability sampling wherein every individual from the targeted
population has an equal chance to be selected in the study. It helps avoid bias and ensures the sample is
representative of the population - much needed by the research to obtain generalizability of the results.

The sampling frame included a list of all professionals and educators that had been engaged for
service in the province of Punjab, so this sample is random and thus includes professions from various
sectors and experience in all geographic directions of Punjab.

### 3.5 Sample Size Calculation

In this analysis, there has been an application of statistical power calculation for establishing
sample size determination. Very essential to it has been the issue of sample size needing to be a number
capable of establishing such differences or relationship which is both valid and meaningful to the study.
To achieve this end, a margin of error at 5 percent and a 95 percent level of confidence are standard
benchmarks, and these figures were adopted in this study. Based on the above parameter, the sample
size for this type was rounded down to approximately 400 participants. However, since the population
is heterogeneous and a probable response rate is assumed, 500 respondents were targeted for the
survey so that at least some complete responses could be returned for analysis.

sample and the wide coverage of questions, the questionnaires were provided through online media as

5. Discussion

This section identifies practical implications for designing AI systems and professional well-
being and sets out the limitations of the study so that directions of future research could be formulated
based on these study results in light of earlier literature. The discussion is the final part of analyzing
data presented above in the last sections in so far as those relate to cognitive and emotional
consequences of AI dependency on health professionals and educators of Punjab.

The findings of this research offer the most striking psychologies effects arising from the
presence of AI on implementation, primarily within high stakes professional settings to the healthcare
as well as learning settings. From a factual stand, results as portrayed by correlation analysis reveal
tremendous interplay across use of AI along both factors in terms of use and span: AI reliance has
deeper psychic influences on overall well-being, truly.

A positive correlation with the time that AI is in use and mental overload means the experience
of being mentally exhausted, probably with extended periods of the use of an AI system; the value being
positive at 0.44 (p < 0.01). This is consistent with Durosini et al. (2024), who also made a similar
connection between long-term use of technology and cognitive strain because overuse of AI-based tools
may overwhelm people's working memory and attention capacity. On the other hand, Nizamani et al.
(2024) take this argument to the next level when they claim that AI tools which are developed without
consideration on the cognitive load can inhibit professionals' decision-making processes and can cause
decision fatigue.

The relation is also very positive towards the frequency of AI usage as a stressor and emotional
stress (r = 0.51, p < 0.01). This is at the psychological level, leading to more frequent usage of AI tools
as a stressor. According to Patnaik & K, K. P. (2024), the use of AI in health care results in emotional
load on health care professionals, hence leading to a burnout feeling among them. The dependents get
exposed to too much dependence of the professionals on the AI systems at critical moments of
decisions, thus bound to raise their anxiety and levels of stress toward the professionals. Even
Bittencourt et al. (2024) stated that emotional stress in using AI tools rose with their use as black
boxes, whose decision processes could not be quite understandable, further raising psychological stress.

With the frequent use of AI, there is a negative correlation between the frequency of usage of AI

human judgment and interaction by technology.

The implication of the correlation results is that whereas there is obvious gain from the
introduction of AI, it is equally destructive psychologically that cannot be ignored. In fact, its use was
established to be positively related to the level of cognitive overload and emotional stress. It therefore
calls for AI at work to be very well implemented so that it can supplement human abilities and not
substitute them.

### 5.1 Applied Implications

Well regarded findings of this study have a good deal of practical implications on the design and
use of AI systems, especially in the realms of healthcare and education. As it can be understood, with
high stakes, there is a sheer requirement of user-centered design in AI systems, especially in the realms
of health care and education settings. The results point to the need to create AI tools that alleviate both
cognitive overload and emotional strain. Adaptive AI systems that adapt to user skills and preferences
may turn out to be the way to go with regards to reducing levels of stress. For this purpose, AI systems
must be designed in such a way that the underlying rationale of the AI-based recommendations
provided to it can easily assuage the anxiety that technologies are entitled to make decisions as argued
by Vazquez et al. (2022). Second auxiliary argument by Schlimbach et al. (2024) is that, at least there
should be scope for creating feedback loops for the AI systems in such a way that the users can fine-
tune their application appropriately; thereby keeping themselves emotionally less dependent.

Both of these impacts that are, on one hand, cognitive and on the other, emotional ones have to
be managed in health and learning contexts appropriately through proper education and professional
training. This ease the anxiety caused by dependence on technology when AI literacy programs are
implemented to educate the public on what AI can and cannot do. Added urgency: to develop worker
stress management programs, especially in high-pressure jobs in health care. According to the authors,
as in Chung et al., 2023, the institution may be carrying on a routine checkup and access psychological
programs of those employees who are suffering from stress and burning situations because the users of
AI can have access through mental health programs by emphasizing their vital support. That this is

### 5.2 Limitations

Although the current study provides some useful information in relation to the psychological

implications of AI, it still has some limitation:

 Although the 500 sample size was sufficient enough to capture meaningful patterns, it was
limited only to professionals belonging to the province of Punjab. Thus, it is not possible to
generalize findings with other professional people belonging to other provinces and countries,
which would increase validity and further contribute to it becoming a richer understanding of
how AI has affected psychology across diverse contexts.

 Self-reporting questionnaires are likely to be biased because participants might over- or
underestimate their emotional and cognitive responses to AI tools. Future studies can use mixed
methods involving objective data and self-reports to give a more holistic view of AI impacts.
 As the research is of the cross-sectional type, generalizability toward causality could not be
given. Longitudinal research can be applied for the interpretation of long-term psychological
impacts by dependency on AI; how that effect changes may be understood across the time
streams.

### 5.3 Future Work

Future studies would be oriented in overcoming those deficiencies of the current study by

further exploring a few crucial areas such as:

 Longitudinal studies will shed light on how psychological impacts of AI change over the long

haul and what happens over time about AI dependency upon professional well-being.

 Studies with a more heterogeneous population of professionals, based all over the world in
diverse sectors, will be able to better represent the overall understanding of cognitive and
emotional outcomes due to AI.

 More research studies can be integrated with qualitative methods, such as interviews and focus
groups, to gain more insight into the detailed experiences of professionals working in AI
environments. This would provide more information than self-reports can give about how AI
impacts emotional responses and cognitive processes.

 Future work would investigate demographic disparities such as age differences, lack of
experience and education, through which impact on cognition and emotions in healthcare and
education AI could be determined; contrasts between young and older interactions and between
older, experienced professionals would be made.

6. Conclusion

The objective of this study was to investigate the cognitive and emotional effects of AI
dependence among health professionals and educators in Punjab, Pakistan; hence, it elaborated on the
psychological consequences of dependence on artificial intelligence tools in a professional setting. The
results that appeared were quite complex in the interaction between dependence on AI resources and
mental well-being. One of the major findings was that prolonged usage of AI was highly related to
cognitive overload. Results were such that the more time spent on interaction with AI systems, the
higher was the probability of mental fatigue and reduced capacities for effective decision-making. The
results indicated that with the growing dependence of professionals on AI in support of their work,
mental capacity for multitasking and the interpretation of output from AI would be overwhelmed. This
resulted in the inability to process information and make decisions in high-stakes fields such as
healthcare and education, fast-paced environments. A highly significant finding that the frequency of

a higher stress level if they were using tools based on AI more frequently. This was all the more
pronounced in the very environments where they depended on AI in making most decisions. Such
pressure towards relying on AI for proper recommendations or results also added stress to
professionals due to the sense of responsibility they felt in such issues about the accuracy of the output
AI generates. For instance, using AI diagnostic tools or treatment recommendations in healthcare added
stress when professionals feel pressured to trust the systems but remain responsible for the outcome of
the result. Likewise, the teaching professionals who are using AI for grading and assessments showed a
higher stress level because they felt AI is the authority tool to grade the work of their students. Negative
correlation between usage of AI and attention span has also come to the fore. People who highly used AI
tools have reported that they have lesser attention spans which indicate that dependency on AI systems
leads to downfall in sustained cognitive engagement. The continuous interaction with AI systems,
especially during tasks that consisted of repetitive processes or automation, appeared to diminish the
capacity to concentrate on complex or creative tasks. This short attention span was notably evident in
learning institutions since teachers had to maintain their focus on the diverse needs of their clients and
thought critically, two activities that may get easily affected by over-reliance on AI in handling
administrative work.

7. Practical Recommendations

To overcome the negative psychological

impacts

identified by the study, there are

recommendations to be undertaken both at the organizational and individual levels:

 AI tools should be made to fit the cognitive powers of professionals. Rather than bombarding
users with information, AI systems should facilitate decision-making in a manageable format
from which to draw. Developers must take more interest in producing AI interfaces that reduce
mental workload, including intuitive user interaction along with real-time feedback mechanisms
that help professionals navigate through them.

 Healthcare and education professionals require training that goes beyond the mere use of AI
tools but covers the psychological implications of AI usage. Training programs should include
modules on cognitive load management, stress reduction techniques, and how to cope with the
emotional dependency that may develop with frequent AI use. Such training may help reduce the
risks of burnout and cognitive fatigue.

 Regulate the frequency and time of using an AI tool; do not overburden professionals with
cognitive load and emotional stress. Organizations can enforce breaks from screens and AI
systems for frequent intervals. Developing guidelines on AI usage, such as prohibition against
abused engagement in interfaces for extended periods or forcing scheduled downtime, can
protect the mental well-being of employees.
