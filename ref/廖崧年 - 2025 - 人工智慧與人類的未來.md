## 摘要

本文基於尤瓦爾·諾亞·赫拉利在其著作《人類大歷史》中關於人工智慧的論點。赫拉利論點的獨特之處在於，他認為即使是弱人工智慧也足以取代人類，而大多數人則認為需要強人工智慧或通用人工智慧才能取代人類。我將提出三個可能反駁赫拉利的論點，但最終都將失敗。接下來，我將把討論擴展到通用人工智慧。布萊恩·坎特韋爾·史密斯、約布斯特·蘭德格雷貝和巴里·史密斯對此持反對意見。他們的論點適用於強人工智慧，但在弱人工智慧方面則不然。最後，我將基於黑格爾關於有限性和無限性的哲學提出一個論點，該論點可以駁斥赫拉利的弱人工智慧足以取代人類的觀點。

赫拉利認為，即使是弱人工智慧也足以分析人類行為，並且在擁有足夠的數據後便能更準確地預測人類的行為。數據越多，預測就越準確；隨著預測變得越來越準確，人們就會越來越少地自己做出決定，而是選擇遵循人工智慧做出的預測。一旦你把所有的決策都交給人工智慧，人類的價值就所剩無幾了。人工智慧如何在沒有首先獲得理解和意識的情況下分析並預測人類的行為？赫拉利認為，答案不在於電腦科學，而在於生物學。生物學家發現，人類深受基因的影響，並通過對不同因素的複雜計算做出決定。這意味著人類可以被視為通過一系列計算來回應世界的演算法。如果人類是演算法，那麼人工智慧只需要分析人類如何作為演算法進行這些計算，就能預測他們的下一步行動。人工智慧對人類的預測不必完全準確，如果它們的準確性高於人類自身的判斷，那麼人類就應該合乎邏輯地遵循人工智慧的預測，而不是自己的判斷。理解和意識不是必需的，因為人工智慧所做的只是分析人類過去的行為，並用它來預測他們未來的行為。

第一個論點是賽爾著名的中文房間論證。第二個論點來自托馬斯·福克斯，他在其論文〈自戀抑鬱的科技〉中也批評了赫拉利的論點。第三個論點要麼是否認人類必須遵循人工智慧的預測，要麼是規範人工智慧的發展。賽爾的論證可以分為幾個部分。想像一下你身處一個充滿符號和符號操作規則的房間裡。一組符號進入房間，你按照給定的規則操作房間裡的符號來做出回應。你不知道的是，進入房間的那組符號是一個中文問題，而你做出的回應是一個完美的中文答案。在這種情況下，你理解中文嗎？賽爾的答案是否定的，這表明理解不能僅僅通過符號的操作獲得，而這正是人工智慧所做的。托馬斯·福克斯反對赫拉利關於人類可以被簡化為僅僅是演算法的說法。他認為，人類是具有生物身體和感受能力的生命體，而人工智慧缺乏這兩者。為了抵制人工智慧的誘惑，人類必須擁抱他們有限的生物身體並對此感到滿足。第三個論點很簡單，人類可以簡單地選擇不遵循人工智慧的預測，或者我們可以像馬蒂亞斯·里塞建議的那樣規範人工智慧的發展。即使人工智慧為我們提供了最佳解決方案，我們人類也可能更喜歡不太理想的選擇。例如，人工智慧可能會根據一個人的偏好以及期待觀看這部電影幾個月的事實，預測他會觀看某部動作片。然而，我們仍然可以在最後一刻改變主意，簡單地選擇不觀看這部電影。

這三個論點將被證明不足以反駁赫拉利的論點。因此，要解決弱人工智慧通過準確預測取代人類的問題，就必須找到隱藏在人類是演算法的前提中可以推出人類不只是演算法的特徵。這樣的特徵可以在黑格爾關於有限性和無限性的哲學中找到。根據黑格爾的觀點，人類是有限的存在，但他們也超越了自己的有限性，達到了無限。無論弱人工智慧變得多麼先進，它都無法超越自身的有限性，達到無限。

總之，尤瓦爾·諾亞·赫拉利提出了一個有趣的論點，即弱人工智慧足以取代人類。針對強人工智慧的論點在這種情況下是不充分的，因為赫拉利並沒有聲稱人工智慧可以獲得理解或意識。解決弱人工智慧問題的方案是找到隱藏在人類是演算法的前提中足以證明人類不只是演算法的特徵。這個隱藏的特徵是人類超越個體有限性的能力。

## Abstract

> ⚠️ 注意：本論文正文（緒論至參考文獻）以**英文**撰寫，以下為英文 Abstract 及各章節內容。

This thesis critically examines the arguments presented in Yuval Noah Harari's _Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow_. First, there is a difference between weak AI and strong AI. Weak AI specializes in a certain field of work, while strong AI can operate across domains. What makes Harari's argument different is that he argues that weak AI is enough to replace humans, when most argue that strong AI or artificial general intelligence—which usually gains understanding or consciousness—is required to replace humans.

Three possible counter-arguments to Harari are presented but ultimately shown to fail. The discussion is then extended to artificial general intelligence. Brian Cantwell Smith, Jobst Landgrebe and Barry Smith oppose the likelihood of artificial general intelligence. Their arguments are strong against strong AI but not so much for weak AI.

Harari believes that even weak artificial intelligence will be able to analyze human beings as algorithms and make better decisions for humans than humans themselves. With more data the more accurate the predictions, and as the predictions become more accurate the less people would make decisions for themselves, instead opting to follow the predictions AI has made. Once you leave all the decision making in the hands of AI, there is little to no value left for humanity.

The solution proposed is to find what is hidden in the assumption that humans are algorithms that leads to the conclusion that humans are not merely algorithms after all. Such features can be found in Hegel's _Science of Logic_. Humans are finite and limited beings, but they also transcend their finitude and reach into infinity. Weak AI no matter how advanced it becomes cannot transcend its finitude and reach infinity.

In conclusion, Yuval Noah Harari brings up an interesting argument that weak AI is enough to replace humans. Arguments that are directed towards strong AI are insufficient. The solution to the problem of weak AI is to find a hidden feature in the assumption that humans are algorithms that proves humans are not merely algorithms—that they can transcend the finitude of the individual.

## Introduction

This thesis critically examines the arguments presented in Yuval Noah Harari's _Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow_ about the trajectory of humanity in the face of advancing technology, particularly artificial intelligence (AI). While his assertions are compelling, there exist several counterarguments that warrant consideration.

The Chinese Room argument is frequently invoked to challenge the notion of thinking AI. However, this argument is insufficient. Harari's arguments do not hinge on the premise of AI achieving true comprehension; rather, they underscore the implications of AI's capabilities on human society.

Thomas Fuchs raises an argument which directly contests Harari's perspectives. Nevertheless, his approach fails to account for the pervasive influence of AI on humanity as a collective entity, rather than merely focusing on individual experiences.

In the subsequent section of the thesis, the discussion delves into criticisms of artificial general intelligence. The aim is not to dismantle Harari's conclusions, but to show a significant flaw that undermines his entire thesis. Consequently, an alternative perspective rooted in Hegelian philosophy concerning finitude and infinity is proposed. Humans will not lose their intrinsic meaning to artificial intelligence and algorithms precisely because of their finite nature, which inherently constrains the AI they create. In contrast, humanity continually transcends these limitations, with successive generations striving to realize their fullest potential.

The discourse surrounding artificial intelligence has gained unprecedented attention. One can identify two distinct realms: specialized artificial intelligence, designed for specific tasks, and artificial general intelligence, capable of performing any intellectual endeavor akin to a human. Currently, the landscape of AI is dominated by specialized systems, yet technology companies worldwide are ambitiously pursuing the development of general artificial intelligence. This pursuit raises widespread fears of a future where general AI could entirely supplant human roles. However, Harari provocatively suggests that the advent of specialized AI is already sufficient to displace human contributions in various sectors.

In conclusion, while Harari's commentary on the future of humanity in the age of AI is undeniably thought-provoking, it invites a deeper examination of the assumptions that underpin his arguments. As we navigate this complex terrain, it is imperative to critically engage with both the promises and perils posed by AI, recognizing the unique qualities of humanity that technology cannot replicate.

## Literature Review

### Harari's _Homo Deus_

_Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow_ explains how humans got to the point we are at now and predicts what will happen next to this species. Tens of thousands of years ago humans lived relatively stable lives. Then came the agricultural revolution which greatly increased the total output of humans, leading to the construction of cities and giant buildings. However, humans also found themselves trapped in webs of religion, serving different imaginary gods.

Science, capitalism, and humanism create modern society as we know it. But Harari points out that the path we are taking might eventually destroy humanism. The rise of artificial intelligence combined with new discoveries in biology poses an existential threat to humanism. Various research points out that humans are nothing but biological algorithms.

Harari highlights two humanist assumptions being threatened:

1. The individual as a unified being with a single inner voice: _"I am an in-dividual, that is, I have a single essence that cannot be divided into parts or subsystems."_ (_Homo Deus_, p. 382)
2. That only I have access to my inner space of freedom.

In contrast, biology reveals that humans are not individuals but _"an assemblage of many different algorithms lacking a single inner voice or a single self"_ (_Homo Deus_, p. 383). An external algorithm could theoretically know a person much better than they can ever know themselves.

### The Chinese Room Argument (Searle)

Imagine you are in a room with many pieces of Chinese symbols and a set of rules that tell you how to manipulate them. As it turns out, the set of rules allows one to answer questions in Chinese. From the viewpoint of an outsider, the answers produced are just as good as an affluent Chinese speaker. However, this proves that computers cannot understand—following sets of formal rules (syntax) is different from what the symbols mean (semantics). Searle presented this argument to refute the claim that artificial intelligence could achieve understanding.

### Thomas Fuchs

Fuchs argues that humans are living beings with a biological body and the ability to feel—both of which artificial intelligence lacks. People project their ideal self-image onto artificial intelligence they have created. To reject the temptation of AI, humans must embrace their limited biological body and be content with it:

> _"It is the concrete, bodily relationship to others and our embeddedness in an environment of living that can help us to develop a realistic and life-promoting self-image."_

### Mathias Risse

Risse argues in _Political Theory of the Digital Age: Where Artificial Intelligence Might Take Us_ that artificial intelligence with its immense power should not be owned by a few private companies; instead it should be supervised as a public infrastructure. A third possible argument is to either reject the idea that humans would simply follow AI advice, or restrict the development of AI with checks and balances.

### Brian Cantwell Smith

Smith distinguishes between two forms of thinking: **reckoning** and **judgement**. He states that _"neither first-wave AI, nor any proposals yet advanced for third wave, will lead to genuine intelligence. Systems currently being imagined will achieve formidable reckoning prowess, but human-level intelligence requires judgement."_ (_The Promise of Artificial Intelligence_, Introduction)

Reckoning is the ability to make calculations, and artificial intelligence can be very powerful at doing calculations. But judgement is the ability to care about what will happen and take responsibility for our actions—requiring ethical commitments and concern for the world.

There is the first wave of AI based on the theory that thinking is just performing symbolic operations on well-defined object symbols according to a set of formal rules. While the first generation could play chess or prove theorems, it fails at simple tasks like recognizing pictures of cats. The second generation solves this through machine learning, utilizing statistical inferences to analyze patterns.

### Jobst Landgrebe and Barry Smith

Landgrebe and Smith aim to prove that artificial general intelligence cannot be done. Their argument is that the human neurocognitive system is a complex system that is impossible to build mathematical models for:

> _"The most striking in neuroscience, which is the science dealing with the physical explanation of how the brain functions in giving rise not only to consciousness, intelligence, language and social behavior but also to neurological disorders such as dementia, schizophrenia."_ (_Why Machines will Never Rule the World_, Introduction)

To build artificial general intelligence, a mathematical model of the human brain that could predict the human system is required. Since said model is impossible to build, artificial general intelligence is also impossible.

### Hegel's _Science of Logic_

For Hegel, philosophy must begin without any presuppositions. In the beginning of philosophy there are none of the concepts or ideas we are familiar with—then there was indeterminate being. But indeterminate being is so purely indeterminate it becomes indistinguishable from nothing. Nothing is, and thus it transits back to indeterminate being. The back-and-forth transition between being and nothing is **becoming**.

Determinate being is what it is and not what it is not—both real and negative at the same time. Determinate being changes into **something**. Something is defined by its limit: it can be itself only if there is something else other than it. But the in-itself of something negates the limit, and so something becomes **finite**.

The finite comes to its end on its own accord. After it arrives at its end, what is left is not simply nothing—one finite thing turns into another finite thing endlessly. As a finite thing passes away, it fulfills its determination and is what it is. Through coming to their end, finite things turn out to be unending—the **infinite**.

However, as long as the infinite holds on to its opposition to the finite, the infinite is limited by the finite, making it a "bad infinite." **True infinity** lies not in the opposition between finite and infinite, but in the unity of the two—an ongoing process wherein being reconciles with itself.

## Research Motivation and Purpose

When I first delved into Yuval Noah Harari's _Homo Deus_, I was struck by a vision of the future that was as horrifying as it was thought-provoking. The prospect of a bleak existence where humans lose all relevance and meaning compelled me to reflect on the role philosophers must play in navigating this impending crisis.

My initial reaction was an instinctive skepticism towards Harari's arguments. While it is common for philosophers to assert that humans can be distilled down to mere biological algorithms, I find this assertion to be an oversimplification, failing to capture the rich complexity of human existence.

If we concede that we are merely biological algorithms, we simultaneously unlock the potential for limitless upgrades. We could modify our genetic makeup, integrate with computers, or even evolve into silicon-based beings. Moreover, our inner voice—the very core of our identity—would not be extinguished. If we accept the premise that humans are algorithms, then our inner voice could simply be a highly advanced computer executing complex software, capable of generating meaning and purpose for the world.

The demise of humanity as limited biological algorithms does not stem from being overshadowed by artificial intelligence, but from the inherent finitude in their very nature. Homo Sapiens, as biological constructs, may come to an end, but in that very dissolution, a new humanity rises from the ashes. Homo Deus, the new face of humanity, diverges markedly from Homo Sapiens; yet the finite does not vanish into nothingness—it reunites with itself throughout this transformative process.

The objective of this paper is to argue that advancements in artificial intelligence will not only amplify our inner voice but will also represent a profoundly beneficial path forward wherein we assume that humans are, indeed, algorithms. Instead of being confined to a tiny blue dot named Earth, we could traverse the cosmos, gathering experiences that are currently beyond our comprehension.

## Argument

### Addressing the Chinese Room

Even if AI operates without understanding, users can still expect satisfactory responses to their queries. In a world driven by efficiency, the average consumer is unlikely to prioritize whether a task is accomplished through authentic comprehension or sheer processing prowess. Artificial intelligence does not need to possess understanding in order to exert influence over humans.

### Addressing Thomas Fuchs

Fuchs contends that we must reject the notion that humans can be distilled to mere algorithms, yet simultaneously urges acceptance of our biological limitations. This stance is fundamentally flawed, as it overlooks the intrinsic nature of humanity: an unwavering drive to become what we ought to be. Humans are fundamentally driven by a desire to transcend their limitations; the acknowledgment of one's constraints simultaneously ignites a yearning for something greater.

### Addressing the "Just Ignore AI" Argument

From the point of view of thinkers who deliberate on the topic, the solution to ignore artificial intelligence seems obvious. However, for the average person, AI provides plenty of benefits and very little downside. When AI provides convenient recommendations, people will increasingly follow them. Even those who resist individually face a collective action problem:

> Those who give up the power of artificial intelligence will not be able to compete with those who embrace it. So, everyone is forced to chase after artificial intelligence regardless of their opinion on the subject.

Banning AI outright steps into the territory of totalitarianism, while allowing unrestricted use enables dangerous concentration of power.

### Addressing Brian Cantwell Smith

Harari is pointing out what artificial intelligence **can** do, not what it **ought** to do. In a perfect world AI will only be used for benevolence, yet in the real world it would be difficult to avoid the technology falling into the wrong hands. For example, AI in hospitals could lead to more accuracy and efficiency, but also gives totalitarian governments a chance to collect personal data. Autonomous weapons could lead to the destabilization of the world.

### Addressing Landgrebe and Smith on AGI

A critical distinction must be acknowledged: the type of prediction Landgrebe and Smith address demands absolute accuracy, while Harari's perspective allows for a more pragmatic approach. Harari's vision is not of an omnipotent AI capable of flawless predictions, but rather of an evolving system that enhances its predictive accuracy over time.

AI has many tools and tricks available:

- **Search engine data**: Algorithms already control which results show up, allowing AI to influence public opinion through filters on what is deemed relevant.
- **Social media algorithms**: Content promotion and restriction determines what users receive, creating custom echo chambers.
- **Historical influence tactics**: Propaganda, gaslighting, and peer pressure are common tactics used to great effects.

### The Humanism Argument

The reason every opinion is treated as equal in humanism is because there is no inherent meaning in this world. Hence, artificial intelligence cannot replace our inner voice. While it may seem like it is making decisions for us, it cannot create meaning in this world. Humans remain the ultimate source of meaning.

### The Hegelian Solution: Paradox and True Infinity

Artificial intelligence interprets the world in a rigid manner. If proposition A is true, it cannot simultaneously be false. This binary logic starkly contrasts with the nuanced ways in which humans engage with thought, particularly paradoxes and contradictions.

Consider Hegel's beginning: indeterminate being dissolves into nothingness, yet nothing is not merely nothing—it transitions back into indeterminate being. Artificial intelligence struggles to process such complexities. If AI were to address Hegelian thought independently, it would face insurmountable challenges. The crux lies in AI's inability to utilize disjunctions effectively:

> To comprehend Hegelian thought, AI would need to recognize that being is not merely finite under certain conditions and infinite under others, but rather that being encompasses a simultaneous existence of being and nothing, becoming and determinate being, and so forth.

No matter how many disjunctions can be programmed, they will always be finite, whereas the concept of infinity transcends finite definitions. Artificial intelligence is static, existing simply as it is, devoid of any inherent aspiration.

This fundamental limitation of AI underscores the impossibility of recreating what Hegel describes as **stubborn understanding**—a clear-cut perspective where the finite remains finite and the infinite remains infinite. Unlike AI, we possess the unique capacity to relinquish our stubborn understanding.

### The Overman vs. The Last Man

As finite beings, humans can and will transcend their limits. But there is one last paradox: living forever becomes an eternal cage that binds forever, holding on to the opposition between finite and infinite. To transcend death, one does not live forever—one becomes the overman.

Contrary to the overman, the **last man** holds on to stubborn understanding and dwells in finitude forever. We can choose to become the overman or the last man—it is the easiest decision to make for the cost and the benefits are clear, yet also the most difficult choice, for this means abandoning the familiar world.

### Strengthening Humanism through AI

If improvements are welcomed, then there are many ways AI and other technologies could improve our lives:

1. **Meaning through freedom**: Once AI takes over jobs, humans are truly free to explore meaning. A cook could be thinking about what it means to be a good cook and explore the different ways to achieve this goal.

2. **New experiences**: If we leave difficult missions to AI, humans could link to the Internet and surf the massive void, going from stars to stars. Future technology may allow humans to upload themselves and transform into another life form.

3. **Understanding ourselves**: AI can analyze our biological algorithms and understand us better than we ever could, making better decisions while humans explore their inner voice. If tools cannot generate meaning for the world, they will remain as merely tools.

The bleak world created by modern society will be repainted in unimaginable colors, and the victory of humanism is secured.

## Conclusion

Harari is right to point out the incoming artificial intelligence revolution that will change our lives. But he made a pessimistic prediction on the assumption that humans can be reduced to merely biological algorithms. Even if we hypothetically accept that we are biological algorithms, we find that humans are always something more than mere algorithms—so artificial intelligence will not make humans useless.

Modern society has already given humans immense powers, enabling them to overcome famine, plague, and war. Yet, humans traded meaning for such power. However, artificial intelligence allows us to surpass our limitations. With help from powerful external algorithms, we can understand ourselves better than ever. If we are the only ones capable of creating meaning in this world, artificial intelligence will not replace us but only make us stronger.

We learn from Hegel that humans are finite beings who ought to be more. Artificial intelligence cannot replace us because it is never more than what it is, and neither can it make the choice for us since it is incapable of making this decision.

> Artificial intelligence cannot replace humanity. Unlike AI, we possess the unique capacity to relinquish our stubborn understanding. It is this ability to let go of tightly held beliefs and rigid frameworks that distinguishes us from artificial intelligence.

Concerns about the danger of artificial intelligence have been discussed many times in modern times. But Harari makes a new argument that artificial intelligence could threaten the intrinsic values of humans without even having consciousness. However, artificial intelligence in its current form will never threaten the core value of humanism—our unique ability to create meaning in this world. Instead, we could use artificial intelligence as a tool to enhance our meaning-giving capability and open new territories for humans to explore.

> What will jeopardize our future is not the technology we invented, but we ourselves who used it.
